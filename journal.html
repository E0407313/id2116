WEEK 01

The object that we have chosen for our project is a traditional fan.


Lester's 'antique' wooden fan

We chose a fan due to its interesting transformations. And also because we thought it is beautiful. Firstly, its ability to expand and contract with a simple flicking motion using one's hand. We noticed how every person has their own unique way of handling a fan: some open it slowly, others with two hands.

Secondly, the movement that we make when utilizing the fan to cool ourselves also allows the fan to generate wind. The act of fanning can also be used to divert wind currents.

Thirdly, due to the interesting structure of the fan , we can conduct stress tests to determine its flexibility.

Last but not least, light that passes through a fan varies depending on how many leaves and ribs overlap.

WEEK 02

For this week, we decided to test out the different sensors present in the microbit. Of all the various ranges of sensors present in the Microbit, we decided to narrow down to 3 main sensors that relate back to the transformative nature of the folding fan.

Exercise 1: Tilt Sensor

For our first experiment, we tested if the micro:bit can sense the tilting of the fan when it is in use. To check if the code worked, we programmed the micro:bit to display a face for each different tilt angle. Through trial and error, we realised that the micro:bit has to be tilted at an acute angle (close to 90 degrees) for it to register our programmed inputs. This posed a problem as it meant the event will only be triggered if the fan was fanned unnaturally.

Micro:Bit Interface

Exercise 2: Accelerometer

In this experiment, we programmed the micro:bit to detect acceleration strength when the user is fanning using the 3g/6g/8g programming blocks. We used the same method to test if the micro:bit could detect the acceleration by using faces to represent each of the 3 acceleration strength blocks. We found out that 8g requires a lot of strength to achieve and the face only shows for a brief moment before it fluctuates between the 3g and 6g readings. This meant that the micro:bit was very sensitive in detecting the changes and it could either work to our advantage or vice versa.

Exercise 2.5: Accelerometer

We tested the acceleration speed of our fan opening by attaching the micro:bit to the guard end piece (whats it called) and flicking the fan open and close. We observed that the micro:bit is unable to detect the fan’s acceleration if we flick it downwards on the z-axis but is able to on a flat surface like the x-axis and the y-axis. This led us to the prospect of using the change in acceleration to play sounds as the fan opens and closes.

Exercise 3: Light Sensor

We also attempted to test for light intensity with the fan via its open and closing motion. For this attempt, we observed that while the micro:bit is able to detect rapid and minute changes in light intensity, we felt that the use for this function was too niche.

WEEK 04

It may sound like a no-brainer, but it was only after more in-depth research when we realised that the folding fans aren’t just for fanning. The appreciation of the fan’s intricate beauty also counts towards the experience.

In some cultures in Southeast Asia, someone’s personality can be reflected through their interactions with the fan. Most commonly, acts of coyness or demureness are demonstrated by fanning nimbly and deliberately. We also found out that the foldable fan is called a sensu '扇子'.

In the Philipines, smiling while closing the fan is a sign of attraction.

This made us rethink our initial application of measuring the acceleration of the fan’s opening. Although the gesture looks slick and cool, this act in itself appears to be brash, thoughtless— seemingly counterintuitive to the intricate form of the fan.

This is also when we decided to pay more attention to the fan’s “fanning quality”. So, we gravitated towards measuring the fan’s fanning rapidness by setting boundaries for the frequency and/or speed of movement. With that, we produced several state transition diagrams.


Our first state transition diagram.

There will be an event triggered by the sensu’s opening from its closed idle state. This will take it to the next phase where the sensu is fanned open. From there onwards, it will cycle through three different states: slow, medium and fast based on the frequency of the fanning movement. When the sensu is closed, it will return to its idle state.


Our second state transition diagram.

With the state transition diagram donne, we brainstormed some applications. These included: matching the speed of the flying to the flapping wings of a bird about to take-off. Activating the moving hand of a good luck cat (maneki-neko '招き猫') to match it’s speed. Reversing the fanning effect to work on the face, so it looks like the face is being blown away in the wind.

WEEK 05


The y-axis does not seem to work.

Our initial code involved the use of the y-axis acceleration (mg) input to detect the fanning motion, and the math equation > to define the values within a state.

However, we realized the x-axis/y-axis/z-axis acceleration (mg) was not the acceleration we had in mind. the micro:bit only detected values when tilted in the direction of aforementioned axes. This was unnatural, as compared to the up-and down motion of fanning. Furthermore, the state transitions prescribed to the values did not seem to work as they were not clearly defined.
The fixed code.

So we decided to make use of the strength acceleration (mg) function, which is used to measure seismographic activity during earthquakes. As the micro:bit is always subject to the earth’s gravity due to this preset, the value ‘-1023’ has to be added to calibrate it. There are also more detailed boundaries on the frequency values, so that there is a range of transition speeds.

WEEK 06

With both the p5.js and micro:bit code prepared, we started thinking of the graphics that will go together with the folding fan’s movement.

We wanted something that was delicate and graceful— something that can float with the wind. This was when we thought of bubbles. Bubbles being produced, as though by magic, by simply fanning the air.


How do we bridge the magic between bubbles and a foldable fan?

We considered mapping the code to a video of bubbles being blown by a person. However, we decided against it as we felt that it would lose its meaning.


Why should we not use videos?

Why show a video of blowing when we may as well blow it with our mouths? Isn’t the purpose of coding supposed to be for making the impossibilities in reality possible in a digital space?

The entire bubble scene in Cinema4D.

The rendered bubble animation.

While we still liked the idea of bubbles being blown, we decided to model and animate and render this in Cinema4D. Even though the task was harder, we thought it was necessary to create a scene— seemingly ephemeral and mystical, to do justice for the beauty of a folding fan.



The rendered bubble animation with rings.

We decided to add rings to constrict the movement of the bubbles. I think this allows us to appreciate the fragility of the bubbles, as it melds and contorts through the rings.

WEEK 07



There were very impressive projects in the showcase, ranging from high-fidelity prototypes to rather unexpected applications of everyday objects. My favourite was probably the monster-scare jar.


Our final project.

It was dismayingly apparent that our project did not stand out as much. However, I’m glad it all came together somewhat in the end, just that it didn’t seem very eye-catching.


The screen resized itself every switch.

While the slow fanning motion triggered single bubbles, anything beyond that acceleration will produce an unpleasant jittery effect. This phenomenon was accompanied with an uncontrollable resizing effect and sometimes even disconnected the micro:bit from the wire. It made no sense to me, as all the videos were of the same dimensions.

In spite of all this, the most heartening and unexpecting thing was my partner’s faith in our project. “I really think this is something unconventional,” he said, of the application we chose. Halfway through the exhibition session, we decided to pull the micro:bit offline and troubleshoot live.
