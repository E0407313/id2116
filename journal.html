The object that we have chosen for our project is a traditional fan. We chose a fan due to four main reasons. Firstly, its ability to expand and contract in size with a simple flicking motion using one's hand. Every person has their own unique way of opening a fan. Some open it slowly, others fast, and maybe a small majority opening it with two hands. Secondly, the movement that we make when utilizing the fan to cool ourselves also allows the fan to generate wind. The act of fanning can also be used to divert wind currents. Thirdly, due to the fan being made of thin wood, we can conduct stress tests to determine its flexibility. And lastly, light that passes through a fan varies depending on how many leaves and ribs overlap.

For this week, we decided to test out the different sensors present in the microbit. Of all the various ranges of sensors present in the Microbit, we decided to narrow down to 3 main sensors that relate back to our item, which were acceleration (including the 3g, 6g, 8g options), light intensity and pitch. Experiment 1 : ( videos with images of code ) For our 1st experiment, we tried detecting if the microbit can sense the tilting of the fan when it is in use. To check if the code worked, we programmed the microbit to display a face for each different tilt angle. Through some trial and error, we realised that the microbit has to be tilted at an acute angle ( close to 90 degrees) for it to register the faces that we programmed into it. That was a problem for use as it would require the user to fan the fan 90 degrees upwards and downwards for us to program an event based on the tilt angle. Experiment 2 : (videos with code) In this experiment, we programmed the microbit to detect acceleration strength when the user is fanning using the 3g/6g/8g programming blocks. We used the same method to test if the microbit could detect the acceleration by using faces to represent each of the 3 acceleration strength blocks. We found out that 8g requires a lot of strength to achieve and the face only shows for a brief moment before its fluctuates between the 3g and 6g readings. This meant that the microbit was very sensitive in detecting the changes and it could either work to our advantage or vice versa. Experiment 2.5 : (video with code) We tested the acceleration speed of our fan opening by attaching the microbit to the end piece (whats it called) and flicking the fan open and close. We observed that the Micro:Bit is unable to detect the fan’s acceleration if we flick it downwards on the z-axis but is able to on a flat surface like the x-axis and the y-axis. This led us to the prospect of using the change in acceleration to play sounds as the fan opens and closes. Experiment 3 : (video with code) We also attempted to test for light intensity with the fan via its open and closing motion. For this attempt, we observed that the Micro:Bit was unable to detect rapid and minute changes in light intensity, suggesting that the use of Micro:Bit’s built-in LEDs are unfeasible for this project.

The appreciation of the fan’s intricate beauty also counts towards the experience. In some cultures in Southeast Asia (Japan and China and even Philippines), someone’s personality can be reflected through their interactions with the fan. Source: facebook.com/tagalikhablog Most commonly, acts of coyness or demureness are demonstrated by fanning nimbly and deliberately.
